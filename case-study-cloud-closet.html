Case study · 02

Cloud Closet

Designing for dual audiences — transforming a beautiful but underutilized feature into a genuine sales enablement tool.

Role

Lead UI/UX Designer

Timeline

6 weeks

Platform

Web, tablet

CC.png · hero overview of the dual-mode interface

The problem

Trinity's Cloud Closet launched with high hopes — a digital catalog where sales reps could showcase their clients' complete purchase history. Initial reception was enthusiastic. Then, three months in, a critical problem emerged: the interface optimized for client presentations was functionally unusable as a sales tool.

Sales reps needed to quickly identify fabric details, check availability, and make recommendations. But the existing 3D garment mockups made fabric patterns impossible to discern, and critical information like fabric codes wasn't accessible. A showpiece, not a tool.

Original interface CCOG.png · the before state

Research

I conducted structured interviews with 8 sales reps who had been using Cloud Closet for 2+ months. Their frustration wasn't with the product — it was with being asked to do two different jobs with one tool.

The client persona

  • Needs visual impact and brand confidence
  • Browsing casually, emotional connection
  • Success = "wow factor"

The sales rep persona

  • Needs quick data access and fabric codes
  • Active consultation, time pressure
  • Success = speed to insight
Research synthesis Research.png · interview findings and persona mapping

Key decisions

01

Single-page toggle, not separate navigation

Sales reps switch contexts mid-conversation — "let me show you what this looks like" to "let me check that fabric code" — in seconds. A toggle preserves scroll position and catalog context. Two separate pages would have broken that flow entirely.

Information architecture Context preservation

02

Fabric swatches become the hero in sales mode

In client mode, large 3D garment mockups are the centrepiece. In sales mode, fabric swatches take that role — large, high-contrast, hyperlinked to inventory. Same data, completely different information hierarchy driven by who's looking and why.

Information hierarchy Mode-driven design

03

Mobile-first for tablet field use

Sales reps predominantly used tablets during client consultations. Touch targets minimum 44×44px, swipe-friendly card layouts, persistent toggle control that never scrolls away, and single-column mobile layout — all designed around how the device actually gets used.

Mobile-first Touch optimization

Outcomes

Adoption increased significantly after launch. Sales reps were actually using the tool during active client consultations — the key behavior that hadn't been happening before.

40%

↑ Use

Field adoption of the tool during live client calls

2 modes

One interface, zero compromises on either persona

Reflections

What worked

  • Framing as a dual-audience problem, not a visual one
  • Toggle over separate pages — preserved workflow context
  • Early stakeholder alignment on the two-persona model

What I'd do differently

  • Test the toggle interaction with clients earlier
  • Build analytics in from day one to track mode usage
  • Define clearer success metrics before launch
